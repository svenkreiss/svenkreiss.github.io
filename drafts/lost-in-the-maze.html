Lost in Chris Dixon’s AI Maze

// under machine learning in Tech    // share on Twitter

I had to read Chris Dixon’s blog post on the Idea Maze for AI Startups a few times to digest it and form an oppinion. It is a thoughtful piece and a framework to do a reality check for a startup that works with ML techniques. His flow chart shows that startups should focus on the most basic aspects of ML. When building an MVP, that makes sense as this is what the “M” stands for. Then he shows options for how a product can grow beyond a MVP and this is where one might disagree. If you think a startup can achieve much more, you should pause and ask whether your idea and team is really that good. In some cases, the answer should easily be: “Yes!”

He states many arguments I agree with (especially on fault tolerant UX for AI systems) and four statements one could challenge:

  • big innovation is coming from large companies and research institutes: One could disagree, but I agree. Even if a startup can produce true innovation, selling the magic as a product on its own is really tough when your name is not Google. It’s different though, when your main business is something else and your innovative AI just makes the product better.
  • data is the only component where AI startups get “proprietary advantage”: here the argument is based on the assumption that there are only two components to an AI problem: algorithms and data. However, every ML technology is based on a model. Parametrizations look funny in the ML way, but still exist. For example, for convolutional networks, the model is encoded in the number of layers, their sizes, how weights are shared, by how much the fields overlap, etc. In the next years, models will grow more complex. The best of those models will also create proprietary advantage.
  • current algorithms are great: Strongly disagree. The only point the current algorithms are great in is producing mediocre outcomes within computing and time constraints. Many of todays “great” algorithms are based on older and more accurate algorithms that are too slow or cannot be applied in a streaming fashion. A lot of accuracy got lost along the way. ML is a young field of research and there will be many advances that bring back previously existing features to the current tool chain in big data. There are a lot of low hanging fruits. Beyond the rediscovery of old features, there is an amazing momentum in ML research that will lead to much more innovation. This argument is connected to the next point.
  • virtually endless computing capabilities: We only have virtually endless computing resources, because researchers only pitch their technologies as being “great” when they consume almost zero computing power. It’s an easy to pitch metric. Most recent advances however are due to increased computing power. I don’t think anyone can estimate the amount of creativity that will get unlocked when practicioners can worry less about computing constraints and think about algorithmic and model improvements.

Chris Dixon finishes by saying that progress particularly in data infrastructure and other technological advances will change this idea maze.

Maybe I should have taken the first turn left and joined Stanford or Google.
